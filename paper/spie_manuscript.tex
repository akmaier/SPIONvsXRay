\begin{abstract}
Superparamagnetic iron-oxide nanoparticles (SPIONs) are used for magnetic drug
targeting, hyperthermia, and cell tracking, but a simple question is often left open:
can the deposited iron be seen in a computed tomography (CT) scan? We answer this by
simulation, starting from a real magnetite SPION formulation and building a
delivered-mass dose model in which a fixed particle mass is spread through an
$8\,\mathrm{cm}^3$ tumor. At realistic loading this leaves only a fraction of a
milligram of iron per milliliter, about ten times below the iodine concentration used for CT enhancement, and
because iron has no usable K-edge in the diagnostic range its contrast is purely
photoelectric and lives at low energy. We simulate the whole particle -- a magnetite
core plus a polyacrylic-acid coating -- with the coating fraction taken per
formulation from their thermogravimetry. We simulate a C-arm angiography
system with a fan-beam short scan, Parker weighting, cosine and Shepp-Logan filtering,
a distance-weighted backprojector, and an always-on water beam-hardening precorrection.
We compare an energy-integrating detector (EID) with a photon-counting detector (PCD),
optimizing the tube voltage, aluminum filtration, and PCD energy bins for iron, and
we score detectability by iron $\Delta$HU, contrast-to-noise ratio (CNR), and the Rose
criterion. We study homogeneous cellular uptake and vascular delivery, and verify in
three dimensions on rabbit anatomy. The optimum is a soft $70\,\mathrm{kVp}$ beam with
$1\,\mathrm{mm}$ of added aluminum, where the photon-counting detector reaches up to
about $1.2\times$ the EID iron CNR (its ideal-observer limit); the lowest detectable
iron at the Rose criterion is about $0.5\,\mathrm{mg\,Fe/ml}$.
Overall, SPIONs sit near the CT detection limit at realistic load, and tumor cell
density is the dominant factor for visibility.
\end{abstract}

\keywords{SPION, iron oxide nanoparticles, computed tomography, photon counting,
detectability, contrast-to-noise ratio, spectral optimization, simulation}

\section{INTRODUCTION}
\label{sec:intro}

Superparamagnetic iron-oxide nanoparticles (SPIONs) are used in many nanomedical
applications, such as Magnetic Drug Targeting (MDT), Magnetic Hyperthermia, cell
labeling for tracking, and Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI). The SPIONs our study is
based on are taken from Heinen et al.\cite{Heinen2026}: polyacrylic-acid (PAA)-coated
magnetite ($\mathrm{Fe_3O_4}$) clusters. The cores are about $8$ and $12\,\mathrm{nm}$,
and they assemble into hydrodynamic clusters of about $70$--$250\,\mathrm{nm}$.

A clinical question follows: once these particles reach a target, can we simply see
them in a routine CT scan? The answer matters for dose planning, for reading
incidental findings, and most of all for image-guided delivery, where one wants to
confirm that particles reached the target without a second imaging
modality\cite{Maier2019}.

At first sight the iron oxide looks promising, because it is denser and higher in
atomic number than soft tissue, although iron's atomic number ($Z=26$) is still modest
compared with iodine. The problem is the amount, since at realistic loading the
delivered iron mass is small: a few milligrams of particles spread through a
cubic-centimeter tumor leave only tenths of a milligram of iron per milliliter,
roughly ten times below the iodine concentrations that give comfortable CT
enhancement. Whether this relatively low amount of iron raises the signal above image
noise is therefore not obvious.

The physics is also unhelpful. Iron has a K-edge at $7.1\,\mathrm{keV}$, far below
the diagnostic window, so there is no K-edge jump to exploit as there is for iodine or
gadolinium. The iron contrast is purely photoelectric, which means it is strongest at
low energy and fades quickly as energy rises. This shapes every result: a soft
spectrum helps, a hard spectrum hurts, and photon counting can only help where
low-energy photons survive the body.

Despite a large SPION literature and a surge of interest in photon-counting
CT\cite{Willemink2018,Faby2015,DeMan2007,Goldmann2026},
there is still little quantitative simulation of SPION CT detectability across modern
detectors, and we address this gap. Our contributions are: (i) a delivered-mass dose
model tied to a real magnetite SPION formulation, with a whole-particle material
model and a per-formulation coating fraction from thermogravimetry; (ii) a
spectrum-and-detector optimization of tube voltage, aluminum filter, and PCD energy
bins for iron; (iii) two biological uptake models -- homogeneous cellular uptake and
vascular fresh injection -- grounded in the published cellular loading; and
(iv) a 3D cone-beam verification on real rabbit anatomy. The full pipeline is open
source at \url{https://github.com/akmaier/SPIONvsXRay} (built on the CONRAD
framework), and every intermediate result is public for audit.

\section{MATERIALS AND METHODS}
\label{sec:methods}

We fix the parts in order: the nanoparticle and dose model, the phantom, the
imaging chain, the two detectors, and the experiments and metrics.
Each choice keeps the $c_{\mathrm{Fe}}=0$ case identical to background, so any
measured contrast is attributable to iron alone.

\subsection{Nanoparticle and delivered-mass dose model}
\label{sec:dose}
We model the whole particle, not iron alone: a magnetite ($\mathrm{Fe_3O_4}$) core
plus a PAA coating (monomer $\mathrm{C_3H_4O_2}$). The iron mass fraction of
magnetite is $0.724$ ($3\times55.85/231.5$), and the bound oxygen is nearly
tissue-equivalent, so it adds only a few percent to the attenuation per gram of iron.
The PAA coating is low-$Z$ ($\mathrm{C/H/O}$) and essentially water-equivalent, so
it is negligible for the X-ray attenuation; we still include its mass so the
reported particle concentration is honest.

We keep two concentrations distinct throughout. The iron concentration
$c_{\mathrm{Fe}}$ (mg~Fe/ml) drives the attenuation, while the whole-particle
concentration $c_{\mathrm{NP}}$ (mg~SPION/ml) is derived from it through the coating
fraction. From an iron concentration we recover the whole-particle concentration by
\begin{equation}
c_{\mathrm{NP}} = \frac{c_{\mathrm{Fe}}}{0.724\,(1-\varphi)},
\label{eq:cnp}
\end{equation}
where $\varphi$ is the PAA coating mass fraction. All contrast numbers are reported
as mg~Fe/ml in the tumor.

The coating fraction is taken per formulation from the respective thermogravimetry
(TGA) data of these previously published particles\cite{Heinen2026}. The inorganic residual is $87.7\%$ for
SPION~I ($12\,\mathrm{nm}$ cores) and $66.4\%$ for SPION~II ($8\,\mathrm{nm}$ cores).
On heating, the magnetite in the residual oxidizes to $\mathrm{Fe_2O_3}$, a mass gain
of about $3.4\%$, and this gain is the same for the maghemite ($\gamma$-$\mathrm{Fe_2O_3}$)
and hematite polymorphs, so the coating-fraction estimate is unaffected. Correcting for
it, the magnetite fraction is $84.8\%$ (SPION~I) and $64.2\%$ (SPION~II), with the
complement being PAA, so $\varphi\approx0.15$ for SPION~I and $\varphi\approx0.36$ for
SPION~II. Each formulation is simulated with its own coating, which gives
$c_{\mathrm{NP}}\approx1.63\,c_{\mathrm{Fe}}$ (SPION~I) and
$c_{\mathrm{NP}}\approx2.16\,c_{\mathrm{Fe}}$ (SPION~II). Adding the coating mass
moves the monochromatic iron $\Delta$HU by only about $3.6\%$ at $\varphi=0.15$, so
the attenuation stays iron-dominated. Table~\ref{tab:comp} lists the composition.

We use a delivered-mass dose model, not a fixed vial concentration. We assume a
deposited SPION dose of $6\,\mathrm{mg}$ out of a total of $10\,\mathrm{mg}$ of the
formulation's administered dose for a tumor volume of $8\,\mathrm{cm}^3$, and scale
with concentration, giving the linear relation
\begin{equation}
c_{\mathrm{Fe}} = 0.0543 \cdot c_{\mathrm{form}} \quad [\mathrm{mg\,Fe/ml}],
\label{eq:dose}
\end{equation}
where $c_{\mathrm{form}}$ is the formulation concentration in mg/ml. The reference
$6\,\mathrm{mg}$ dose gives $c_{\mathrm{Fe}}=0.54\,\mathrm{mg\,Fe/ml}$, and even the
top of the sweep is only about $1\,\mathrm{mg\,Fe/ml}$: by design, we model the
diluted delivered dose in the tumor, not the concentrated vial.

The material attenuation is taken from CONRAD's database. As a cross-check, the
magnetite mass attenuation agrees with an independent NIST-based model to five
decimals, so the physics underlying every result is validated. At a representative
$60\,\mathrm{keV}$, $1\,\mathrm{mg\,Fe/ml}$ raises the attenuation by only about
$6.7\,\mathrm{HU}$, which sets the scale of the study. The iron K-edge sits at
$7.1\,\mathrm{keV}$, far below the diagnostic range, so there is no edge to exploit.
Figure~\ref{fig:physics} shows the linear attenuation of soft tissue, cortical bone,
and iron-loaded tumor, and where the iron contrast lives in
energy.

\subsection{Digital phantom}
\label{sec:phantom}
We use a rabbit-scale, electron-density-style calibration phantom: a
$110\,\mathrm{mm}$ soft-tissue cylinder. It holds the iron-concentration inserts on
a common ring, so that beam-hardening cupping is common-mode across inserts, together
with a cortical-bone rod that acts as a deliberate beam-hardening source and makes the
bone factor meaningful. Each insert is a disk of iron-loaded soft tissue, with iron
added to the tissue matrix by the mixture rule, so a zero-iron insert equals
background exactly. Because the soft tissue is a water-equivalent body material,
the zero-iron baseline stays clean. The tumor is an $8\,\mathrm{cm}^3$
sphere ($r=12.4\,\mathrm{mm}$), and the reconstruction field of view is
$20\,\mathrm{cm}$. Figure~\ref{fig:phantom} shows an axial slice and an example
EID/PCD reconstruction.

\subsection{Fan-beam imaging pipeline}
\label{sec:pipeline}
The reconstruction uses established implementations from the CONRAD
framework\cite{Maier2013,Manhart2013}, in its geometry convention. The fan-beam geometry uses
a source-to-isocenter distance of $750\,\mathrm{mm}$ and a virtual detector at the
isocenter sampled at $\Delta_T = 1\,\mathrm{mm}$, with $500$ views over a minimal
short scan ($180^\circ$ plus fan). The chain is forward projection, Parker weighting,
cosine filter, Shepp-Logan ramp filter, and a distance-weighted fan-beam
backprojector, on a $0.5\,\mathrm{mm}$ isotropic grid. A water beam-hardening
precorrection is \emph{always on}, calibrated so a water cylinder reconstructs flat
to about $0.1\%$; it is not a factor of the study.

Reconstruction runs on the GPU through OpenCL, here on an Apple M1 laptop, about
$850\times$ faster than the CPU path.

\subsection{Detectors: EID and PCD}
\label{sec:detectors}
Both detectors start from the same polychromatic forward model. For each energy $E$
the transmitted photons are $N(E)=N_0\,s(E)\,e^{-\tau(E)}$, with $s$ the normalized
spectrum and $\tau$ the material path integral. Quantum noise is Poisson per energy, drawn on the GPU with a counter-based Philox
random-number generator\cite{Salmon2011} and transformed-rejection Poisson
sampling\cite{Hormann1993}.

The energy-integrating detector (EID) weights photons by energy,
$S=\sum_E N(E)\,E$, and its variance is the exact second moment $\sum_E N(E)\,E^2$,
not a single Poisson draw on the summed signal. The line integral is
$p=-\log(S/S_{\mathrm{air}})$, and a single water precorrection linearizes the
response.

The photon-counting detector (PCD) sorts photons into three energy bins, combined
with a \emph{post-combination matched filter}: a matched-filter weighted sum of the
bin counts, then a single logarithm, then one water precorrection on the combined
spectrum. The weights $w_b \propto S_b/V_b$ are the CNR-optimal weighting that
reaches the ideal-observer ceiling. Because this correction uses the combined
spectrum rather than each bin separately, a small residual beam hardening remains,
which is visible in the noise-free reconstructions. Combining in the count domain before the
logarithm is robust to the photon-starved low-energy bin, whose per-bin logarithm
would diverge when its counts reach zero. This scheme gives about $1.2\times$ the
EID CNR, whereas a per-bin pre-sum correction, in which each bin is corrected on its
own spectrum before the sum, is noisier and gives only about $1.0\times$, so we do
not use it.

\subsection{Experiments}
\label{sec:experiments}
We run five experiments. Table~\ref{tab:params} lists the shared simulation
parameters.

\textbf{E1 -- material and physics validation.} We confirm the magnetite
attenuation against NIST, confirm the iron K-edge is out of range, and confirm the
whole-particle model keeps the attenuation iron-dominated
(Fig.~\ref{fig:physics}, Table~\ref{tab:comp}).

\textbf{E2 -- spectrum and detector optimization.} Iron contrast is photoelectric,
so it favours low energy. We sweep the C-arm tube voltage ($70$--$120\,\mathrm{kVp}$)
crossed with aluminum filtration (Al~$1.0$--$8.0\,\mathrm{mm}$) for both detectors,
end-to-end. Hardening metals (Cu, Sn) only hurt iron contrast, so we drop them. E2
then (a) picks the CNR-optimal spectrum, (b) re-optimizes the PCD three-bin
thresholds for it, and (c) feeds the winner to E3--E5. All later experiments run at
this E2 optimum \emph{and} at a $90\,\mathrm{kVp}$ baseline, for comparison against a
standard C-arm spectrum.

\textbf{E3 -- Study~A, homogeneous cellular uptake.} Iron internalized by tumor
cells gives a roughly uniform distribution. We sample the published
cellular loading (Heinen et al., Fig.~5, B16-F10 melanoma; $0/24\,\mathrm{h}$):
SPION~I-113~nm $8.23/3.78$, SPION~II-115 $3.86/1.52$, II-98 $3.60/1.37$,
II-76 $2.51/0.85\,\mathrm{pg\,Fe/cell}$. Each converts to a tumor iron concentration
through a cell density. Cell density is uncertain, so it is a factor with three
levels ($10^8$, $3\times10^8$, $10^9\,\mathrm{cells/cm}^3$), giving a detectability
band. Each configuration uses its formulation's coating; we also cross the bone and
dose factors. Table~\ref{tab:grid} lists the loading-by-density grid.

\textbf{E4 -- Study~B, vascular / fresh injection.} Freshly injected SPIONs are still
in the blood carrier inside the vessels, before cellular uptake. The contrast is
confined to $150\,\mu\mathrm{m}$ vessels that occupy about $10\%$ of the tumor volume
at a tenfold local concentration, and the vessel-local level is the independent
variable ($5$--$15\,\mathrm{mg\,Fe/ml}$). Because the $0.5\,\mathrm{mm}$ voxel cannot
resolve the vessels, each insert is homogenized to its tumor-mean iron. We then add
the second-order effects the homogeneous model omits: the beam-hardening nonlinearity
from concentrating iron, and the structural noise from the binomial per-voxel vessel
count.

\textbf{E5 -- three-dimensional cone-beam verification.} We verify the result in
three dimensions on real anatomy. We use the post-mortem RabbitCT rabbit
volume\cite{Rohkohl2009} and its C-arm geometry, decoded from the benchmark's
projection matrices ($745\,\mathrm{mm}$ source-to-isocenter, $1196\,\mathrm{mm}$
source-to-detector, $496$ views over $198^\circ$). We forward-project
poly-energetically, insert a material-separated $8\,\mathrm{cm}^3$ SPION tumor, and
reconstruct with cone-beam FDK. E5 also runs at the E2 optimum and the
$90\,\mathrm{kVp}$ baseline.

\subsection{Metrics}
\label{sec:metrics}
We score detectability with three quantities. The iron contrast $\Delta$HU is the
mean insert value minus a local annular background, corrected by subtracting the
zero-iron insert so only iron remains. The CNR is that contrast divided by the
background noise over noise realizations. An insert is detectable when its CNR clears
the Rose criterion\cite{Rose,Tapiovaara1985}; we report both $\mathrm{CNR}\ge3$ and
$\ge5$, from $30$ noise realizations per cell. The CNR is a simple surrogate for
formal model-observer detectability on the reconstructed image\cite{Wunderlich2008}.

\section{RESULTS}
\label{sec:results}

\subsection{E2 spectrum and detector optimum}
\label{sec:res-e2}
Because iron contrast is photoelectric, it should favour a soft spectrum, and the E2
sweep confirms this: CNR falls as tube voltage rises and as added aluminum hardens the
beam, so the CNR-optimal spectrum is the softest feasible one, $70\,\mathrm{kVp}$ with
only $1\,\mathrm{mm}$ of added aluminum. At this optimum the matched-filter PCD reaches
about $1.24\times$ the EID iron CNR by the ideal observer, which is $98\%$ of the
ideal-observer ceiling; for this spectrum the PCD bins re-optimize to edges of
$10.0/37.5/47.5/70.0\,\mathrm{keV}$. The soft optimum also lifts the measured EID CNR
by about $22\%$ over the $90\,\mathrm{kVp}$ baseline. The realized PCD advantage,
however, is spectrum-dependent (Sec.~\ref{sec:res-pcd}): the soft optimum leaves the
photon-counting detector little energy separation to exploit, so its measured edge
over EID is smaller than the ideal-observer figure.
Figure~\ref{fig:e2} shows the CNR surface with the optimum marked, and
Table~\ref{tab:pcdcnr} reports the EID versus PCD CNR.

\subsection{Study~A: homogeneous cellular uptake and the cell-density band}
\label{sec:res-a}
The central result is that SPIONs sit near the CT detection limit at realistic load.
Figure~\ref{fig:cnr_a} plots iron $\Delta$HU and CNR against tumor iron
concentration for both detectors, with the Rose lines and a shaded cell-density
band. The band comes from the three cell-density levels: the same cellular loading
maps to different tumor iron concentrations as density changes, so visibility is
dominated by how densely the tumor is packed. The fresh SPION~I loading
($8.23\,\mathrm{pg\,Fe/cell}$) gives tumor concentrations of
$0.82/2.47/8.23\,\mathrm{mg\,Fe/ml}$ at the three densities, and at high dose it
clears the Rose $\mathrm{CNR}=5$ criterion already at $10^{8}\,\mathrm{cells/cm}^3$,
while at low dose it clears it only from $3\times10^{8}$ upward, so detection is
dose-limited rather than detector-limited. The lowest detectable iron at Rose $\ge5$
is $0.46\,\mathrm{mg\,Fe/ml}$ for the PCD at the optimum ($3\times10^{8}$, high dose)
against $0.75\,\mathrm{mg\,Fe/ml}$ for the EID. At the reference load
($0.54\,\mathrm{mg\,Fe/ml}$) the CNR sits on the Rose boundary, while at the top of
the band the PCD CNR peaks near $100$. Figure~\ref{fig:density} shows a reconstruction
montage across the density levels, and Table~\ref{tab:grid} lists the
loading-by-density grid and Table~\ref{tab:thresh} the detection thresholds.

\subsection{Bone off versus bone on}
\label{sec:res-bone}
The cortical-bone rod is a deliberate beam-hardening source, and we repeat Study~A and
Study~B with the rod present. Bone raises the iron $\Delta$HU a little, because the
hardened beam shifts the local response, but it raises the local noise more, through
residual streaks that the local-background and zero-iron subtraction suppress but do
not remove, so the net effect is a modest CNR drop. In Study~A at the optimum, high
dose, SPION~I fresh, the EID CNR falls from $27.4$ to $18.1$ at
$3{\times}10^{8}\,\mathrm{cells/cm}^3$ ($c_{\mathrm{Fe}}=2.47$) and from $88.7$ to
$54.2$ at $10^{9}$ ($c_{\mathrm{Fe}}=8.23$); the PCD falls from $31.1$ to $25.7$ and
from $102.2$ to $64.7$. Study~B behaves the same: at the optimum, high dose, the EID
CNR falls from $6.2$ to $3.5$ at a $5\,\mathrm{mg\,Fe/ml}$ vessel level and from
$13.4$ to $9.0$ at $15\,\mathrm{mg\,Fe/ml}$. The Rose $\ge5$ thresholds stay in the
$0.4$--$1.5\,\mathrm{mg\,Fe/ml}$ regime, so iron detectability is not qualitatively
changed by bone. Table~\ref{tab:bone} lists the bone off versus on CNR for
representative cells.

\subsection{Study~B: vascular / fresh injection}
\label{sec:res-b}
Real fresh-injection uptake is vascular, not uniform, but the decisive fact is one
of scale. A $0.5\,\mathrm{mm}$ voxel is more than three times wider than a
$150\,\mu\mathrm{m}$ vessel, so each voxel partial-volume-averages many vessels. With
mass conserved, the volume-averaged iron per voxel is unchanged, and the tumor-ROI
contrast matches the homogeneous case. The second-order effects are small: the
beam-hardening nonlinearity is well below $10^{-3}\,\mathrm{HU}$, and the binomial
per-voxel vessel count adds texture that averages out over the ROI, so the ROI-mean
detectability tracks the total delivered iron, as in Study~A. At high dose
the ROI-mean CNR crosses the Rose $5$ line at a vessel-local level of
$5\,\mathrm{mg\,Fe/ml}$ (EID) and $7\,\mathrm{mg\,Fe/ml}$ (PCD); at low dose at
$13$ (EID) and $11\,\mathrm{mg\,Fe/ml}$ (PCD). Figure~\ref{fig:studyb} shows that
vascular uptake is CT-indistinguishable from homogeneous uptake at realistic
resolution.

\subsection{EID versus PCD}
\label{sec:res-pcd}
Photon counting helps, but only partly. The post-combination matched-filter PCD
gives about $1.2\times$ the EID CNR when the low-energy bin carries enough counts, but
at realistic dose that bin is photon-starved through the body, so the gain shrinks.
Averaged over the Study~A grid (bone off), the PCD CNR is about $1.15\times$ the EID
at the optimum and $1.25\times$ at the baseline (mean $\approx1.2\times$), because the
softer optimum lifts the EID more and narrows the relative gap. The per-bin pre-sum
variant gives essentially no gain ($\sim1.0\times$) and is noisier, so it is not used.
Figure~\ref{fig:pcd} shows where the iron contrast lives in energy and the
re-optimized bin thresholds, and the gain sits in a band that receives few transmitted
photons.

\subsection{Three-dimensional verification}
\label{sec:res-3d}
Finally we verify the result in real anatomy. We segment the post-mortem RabbitCT
volume into soft tissue, adipose, and bone, add an iron-loaded neck tumor,
forward-project each material in 3D cone-beam geometry at the E2 optimum, and
reconstruct with cone-beam FDK for both detectors. The iron signal is positive and
scales linearly at about $6.8\,\mathrm{HU}$ per $\mathrm{mg\,Fe/ml}$, matching 2D. The PCD gives a higher CNR at every level, a gain of about $1.16\times$, as
E2 predicts. For SPION~I fresh at a mid cell density ($3\times10^{8}$,
$c_{Fe}=2.47$) the tumor reads $+17/+19\,\mathrm{HU}$ (EID/PCD); at the highest load
($10^{9}$) $+56/+62\,\mathrm{HU}$. The 3D result agrees with the 2D detection limit
(Fig.~\ref{fig:rabbit}).

% =====================================================================
\section{DISCUSSION}
\label{sec:discussion}

The picture is coherent. At realistic load, SPIONs deposit little iron, which
produces only a few Hounsfield units of contrast, and the CNR lands near the Rose
criterion, not comfortably above it. The reference dose is therefore
borderline-visible: detectable in principle at low noise and a soft spectrum, but not
a robust everyday finding.

Which factor helps most? Cell density: the same cellular loading maps to a wide range
of tumor iron concentrations depending on how densely the tumor is packed, so
visibility is dominated by cell density, not the formulation alone. The next lever is
the spectrum, because a soft beam raises the photoelectric iron contrast, while photon
counting gives only a modest $1.2\times$ gain, and only when the low-energy bin
survives the body. Beam-hardening correction does not change iron detectability,
because iron contrast is a genuine material signal, not a cupping artifact.

The study has clear limitations. It is a simulation with idealized, ideal-energy
detectors and no scatter or patient motion. The 2D phantom is a round, rabbit-scale
cylinder rather than full anatomy, and soft tissue is a water-equivalent proxy.
Study~B shows sub-resolution vessels do not change detectability at the same total
dose, and E5 brings in real anatomy, but a full 3D detectability sweep is future
work. The open decisions -- tumor cell density (Study~A) and injection concentration
(Study~B) -- are reported as bands.

% =====================================================================
\section{CONCLUSION}
\label{sec:conclusion}

Can superparamagnetic iron-oxide nanoparticles be seen in CT? At realistic load, only
barely: our simulation places the detection limit near the reference
SPION dose, at a few Hounsfield units and a CNR on the Rose boundary. Tumor cell
density is the dominant factor, while photon counting adds about $1.2\times$ and a
soft spectrum is the most effective lever. Routine CT confirmation of SPION delivery
is therefore feasible only at the upper end of realistic loading, with a spectrum
tuned for iron, and full 3D anatomical detectability and experimental validation are
the next steps.
